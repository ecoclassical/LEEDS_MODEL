Two-Region Macroeconomic Models: Policy Insights, Migration, and Sectoral Scope

Author

Oriol Vallès Codina

1 Overview

Two-region macroeconomic models are widely used to analyze international spillovers, financial imbalances, exchange-rate regimes, and climate policy coordination. This note summarizes policy recommendations/insights, whether models address migration flows, and whether they are single- or multi-sector.

2 1. Backus–Kehoe–Kydland (1992)

Tradition: Real Business Cycle (RBC)
Regions: Home vs. Foreign

Core mechanism: International comovement arises from productivity shocks and trade/financial linkages under frictionless competitive markets (with incomplete asset markets in practice).

Policy insights: - Observed consumption comovements are lower than predicted → risk-sharing is incomplete; financial frictions, home bias, or capital controls matter. - Highlights the need to model incomplete international asset markets for policy-relevant conclusions.

Migration: Not modeled (labor immobile).
Sectors: One representative tradable sector.

3 2. Obstfeld–Rogoff (1995, 2001)

Tradition: Two-country New Keynesian open economy
Regions: Two symmetric/asymmetric countries

Core mechanism: Monopolistic competition, nominal rigidities, pricing-to-market, and international asset trade generate rich policy spillovers.

Policy insights: - Under sticky prices, independent monetary policy may not be globally optimal; policy coordination can raise welfare relative to Nash behavior. - Exchange-rate regime choice interacts with nominal rigidities and home bias; pass-through and pricing-to-market shape optimal rules.

Migration: Not modeled.
Sectors: Typically two (tradables and non-tradables, differentiated varieties).

4 3. Galí & Monacelli (2005)

Tradition: Small Open Economy New Keynesian (derived from a two-country setup)
Regions: Small domestic economy interacting with the rest of world

Core mechanism: Incomplete exchange-rate pass-through, Calvo price-setting, and foreign demand shocks.

Policy insights: - Optimal monetary rule: target domestic inflation and output gap, not the exchange rate; small economies retain monetary autonomy even with high openness. - Provides microfoundations for modern inflation-targeting in open economies.

Migration: Not modeled.
Sectors: One composite good (extensions split into tradables/non-tradables).

5 4. Godley & Lavoie (2007) – Open-Economy SFC

Tradition: Stock–Flow Consistent (SFC) macro with two countries
Regions: Domestic vs. Foreign

Core mechanism: Full accounting of sectoral balance sheets and flow consistency; current account surpluses/deficits matched by opposite financial positions.

Policy insights: - External and internal balances must cohere; persistent current-account imbalances have balance-sheet consequences. - Coordinated fiscal policy can sustain demand without unsustainable debt accumulation. - Warns against export-led mercantilism as a generalized strategy.

Migration: Not explicit in the canonical chapters; extensions add remittances and labor flows.
Sectors: Multi-sector (households, firms, government, banks; often CB).

6 5. Bhaduri–Marglin and Extensions (Onaran, Blecker)

Tradition: Post-Keynesian, demand-driven growth with open economy linkages
Regions: Two economies with interacting demand regimes (wage-led vs. profit-led)

Core mechanism: Income distribution affects aggregate demand, investment, and net exports; spillovers transmit demand regimes across borders.

Policy insights: - Wage suppression abroad can depress global demand; a “race to the bottom” harms growth. - Advocates coordinated wage and fiscal policies to raise global growth and employment.

Migration: Generally not explicit; can be incorporated via labor supply or remittances.
Sectors: Usually one production sector (goods); some extensions separate capital goods/energy.

7 6. Palley (2015) – Structural Keynesian North–South

Tradition: Structural Keynesian with North–South asymmetries
Regions: Core (high-wage, high-productivity) vs. Periphery (low-wage, dependent)

Core mechanism: Asymmetric wage structures, technology gaps, and financial dependence shape trade and growth outcomes.

Policy insights: - Calls for industrial policy, capital management techniques, and labor-market institutions in the South to prevent divergence. - Recommends re-regulating global finance to stabilize external balances.

Migration: Sometimes present as a distributional/bargaining channel.
Sectors: Often two (primary vs. manufacturing).

8 7. Ecological & Unequal-Exchange Two-Region Models

8.1 7.1 D’Alessandro & Luzzati (2014)

Tradition: Ecological macro/structuralist two-region frameworks
Policy insights: Trade-based growth is constrained by environmental limits; recommends Northern demand reduction alongside Southern ecological industrialization and technology transfer.
Migration: Typically not modeled explicitly.
Sectors: Multi-sector (energy/resources + goods).

8.2 7.2 Rezai & Stagl (2016)

Tradition: Climate-policy spillovers in two-region macro
Policy insights: Carbon pricing in the North can worsen Southern terms of trade without redistribution; recommends global sharing of climate rents and transfers.
Migration: Not modeled.
Sectors: Multi-sector (energy + final goods).

9 8. RICE / DICE (Nordhaus)

Tradition: Integrated Assessment Models (IAMs) with multiple regions; two-region variants are common for pedagogy
Core mechanism: Output and emissions feed climate dynamics and damages which feed back to output.

Policy insights: - Decentralized equilibria under-abate emissions; cooperative carbon pricing yields higher global welfare. - Useful for analyzing international climate agreements and burden-sharing.

Migration: Not modeled (population exogenous).
Sectors: Two broad blocks (production and climate/damages), often enriched with energy detail in RICE-type models.

10 9. Mundell–Fleming and Dornbusch Overshooting

Tradition: Open-economy IS–LM (Mundell–Fleming) and sticky-price exchange-rate dynamics (Dornbusch)
Regions: Two-country intuition often used, though canonical models are single small open economy

Policy insights: - With high capital mobility and fixed exchange rates, monetary policy loses autonomy; under flexible rates, stabilization is possible but with exchange-rate volatility. - Forms the backbone of the impossible trinity.

Migration: Not modeled.
Sectors: One composite tradable sector.

12 Notes on Migration Modeling

  • Rare in macro two-region cores; when present, migration is usually exogenous or via partial equilibrium channels (remittances, labor supply).
  • Where to look: development/structuralist models (Lewis, Harris–Todaro), SFC/ABMs adding migration/remittances (e.g., Caiani et al., Dafermos et al.).
  • Frontier direction: endogenize migration decisions linked to wage differentials, climate damages, and policy (industrial policy, carbon pricing), with feedback to sectoral demand and public finance.
