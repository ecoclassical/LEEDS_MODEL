Utility, Conservation, and Falsifiability: Hands, Mirowski, and Modern Revealed Preference

1. Aim and scope

This note ties together Mirowski’s “energy metaphor”, D. Wade Hands’ clarifications on integrability, and the revealed-preference (RP) testing program (WARP/SARP/GARP, Afriat, stochastic RP), to assess whether neoclassical consumer theory is merely “as‑if” or whether it yields empirically falsifiable conservation‑type restrictions.

2. Mirowski’s thesis in brief: conservation as a scientific anchor

Mirowski argues that many sciences lean on a conservation principle to define systems and measure change. Neoclassical economics, he claims, imported from mid‑19th‑century physics a field view in which utility acts like potential energy, encouraging the idea that prices form a conservative vector field (path independence, exact differentials) and implying problematic “conservation” statements such as a constant utility+expenditure sum. fileciteturn3file7L16-L25 fileciteturn3file0L75-L83

Carlson’s survey frames this as an “integrability problem”: if you push the physics analogy literally, you get absurdities (e.g., constant utility+expenditure) and a tension between subjective preference mappings and objective commodity flows—a “mind–body” dualism in the theory’s foundations. fileciteturn3file0L79-L83 fileciteturn3file5L1-L17

Mokyr’s review tempers the critique: physics analogies may be better treated as heuristics rather than justifications—useful for asking questions and borrowing tools, not for importing literal conservation laws. fileciteturn3file11L10-L19

3. Hands’ correction: which “conservation” does integrability buy?

Hands shows that the standard integrability conditions in modern micro (Slutsky symmetry/negativity) do not make Walrasian prices a conservative vector field. Instead, they make the compensated (Hicksian) demand a conservative field whose potential is the expenditure function. Therefore, the “core physics metaphor” only holds in a weaker, compensated sense. fileciteturn3file3L10-L18 fileciteturn3file2L34-L43 fileciteturn3file4L13-L21

Formally, Slutsky symmetry yields path‑independence for the compensated system (not the Marshallian one); Walrasian prices would be conservative only under much stronger (generally false) conditions—e.g., vanishing income effects. fileciteturn3file9L45-L62 fileciteturn3file12L10-L17

Implication. If one insists on a conservation metaphor, the right object is the expenditure/compensated demand system. This also explains why celebrated results tied to a strict conservative structure (e.g., Samuelson’s correspondence principle) fail in general unless one imposes stronger symmetry than Slutsky’s. fileciteturn3file12L27-L37

4. RP makes maximization testable: WARP/SARP/GARP → Afriat → Stochastic RP

4.1 Deterministic RP

  • WARP/SARP/GARP. These axioms forbid cycles in observed choices across budgets. GARP is the workhorse: if a finite dataset satisfies GARP, then it is rationalizable by a monotone, concave utility. (Afriat’s Theorem.) It also delivers the Afriat Efficiency Index, quantifying deviations from exact maximization.
  • Slutsky‑type tests. With price–quantity data, one can test negativity and symmetry (nonparametrically or with flexible demand systems). These are conservation‑style restrictions on substitution patterns.

4.2 Stochastic RP (SRP)

SRP (e.g., McFadden–Richter; Kitamura–Stoye) allows for noise and sampling variability, providing statistical tests of RP (and welfare bounds) when choices are probabilistic. In spirit, SRP relaxes exact conservation into moment inequalities that can be tested with finite samples.

Bottom line. RP is not merely “as‑if”: it imposes sharp inequality systems that are falsifiable in finite datasets and estimable in noisy data.

5. Wong’s critique and the “as‑if” defense

Wong (1978) argued that Samuelson’s revealed‑preference project did not fully escape unobservables or guarantee empirical content; the RP axioms risk tautology or vacuity if applied without domain restrictions, regularity, or aggregation discipline. The “as‑if” defense (Friedman) then looms: models explain by predictive success, not by realism of primitives.

Hands’ and Carlson’s take. Even setting aside Mirowski’s hermeneutics, once you try to literalize the physics analogy, integrability delivers stringent conditions that can be inspected. In practice, modern micro backs away from the strong conservative field for prices and works with compensated systems, precisely to retain testable structure without absurdities. fileciteturn3file0L55-L63 fileciteturn3file8L11-L19

6. Aggregation and the limits of falsifiability (SMD)

At the aggregate level, the Sonnenschein–Mantel–Debreu (SMD) results show that almost any well‑behaved excess demand can arise from some heterogeneity—so tight RP at the individual level does not translate into strong aggregate restrictions. Hence the “it depends on the modeler’s ingenuity” critique does bite at aggregation, even if micro‑level RP is empirically tight.

7. What does Hands’ reading imply for Mirowski and “conserved utility”?

  1. Conservation lives in Hicksian space. The mathematically defensible “conservation” is on compensated (not Marshallian) ground. The potential is expenditure, not utility+expenditure. fileciteturn3file4L13-L21
  2. Falsifiability is real but localized. RP and Slutsky restrictions provide operational content at the individual/market level; SMD weakens cross‑market, aggregate claims.
  3. Heuristic, not dogma. Treat the physics analogy as heuristic (Mokyr), while using its mathematical discipline (integrability, symmetry) to generate testable inequalities. fileciteturn3file11L10-L19
  4. No free lunch for “as‑if.” You can always craft “as‑if” stories, but once you commit to maximization with regularity (monotonicity, concavity), you inherit Afriat/GARP tests and Slutsky structure—empirical commitments that can fail in data.

8. Takeaways for your project on “conservation of value–utility”

  • In the classical (value) story, exchange is conservative; labor/finance are source/sink terms. In the neoclassical (utility) story, conservation appears most coherently in compensated demand (path‑independent expenditure). That is where the “field” metaphor runs cleanly.
  • If your paper argues that neoclassical economics “assumes conservation of total utility,” the precise target should be compensated systems (or very restrictive cases with vanishing income effects). Otherwise, utility+expenditure conservation is exactly the “absurdity” Mirowski criticized. fileciteturn3file0L79-L83
  • Empirics to run: (i) GARP/Afriat with efficiency indices; (ii) nonparametric Slutsky negativity/symmetry on Hicksian data; (iii) stochastic RP tests to assess statistical compliance.
