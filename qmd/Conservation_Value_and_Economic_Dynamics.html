Conservation of Value and Economic Dynamics

1. Overview

This paper connects three lines of analysis:

  1. The principle of conservation of value in exchange and the labor theory of value in classical political economy.
  2. The Hamiltonian structure of macroeconomic dynamics in the models of Goodwin and Flaschel–Semmler, representing conservative systems of value circulation.
  3. The dissipative cycles of financialized economies, exemplified by Foley (1987), in which credit and liquidity introduce nonconservative forces.

The argument is that value conservation in exchange plays a role analogous to energy conservation in mechanics. Production and finance then appear as the source and dissipative terms that break this conservation.

2. The Classical Principle of Value Conservation

2.1 Value in exchange

In classical political economy, exchange is conservative: it redistributes value but does not create it. In pure circulation,

\[ \sum_i p_i \Delta x_i = 0, \]

so the total value of commodities remains constant. Only production, through the application of living labor, generates new value:

\[ p^\top x = p^\top A x + wL, \quad \Rightarrow \quad p^\top (I-A)x = wL. \]

Here, \(A\) is the input–output matrix and \(wL\) the newly created value. This identity expresses a continuity equation of value:

\[ \frac{dV}{dt} = \text{Value created by labor} - \text{Value destroyed by consumption or depreciation}. \]

2.2 Relation to classical mechanics

In physics, conservation of energy follows from time-translation symmetry (Noether’s theorem). In economics, value conservation arises from exchange symmetry—the equivalence of commodities in value terms. Labor breaks this symmetry by introducing a source term in the continuity equation.

Thus, the classical system can be viewed as a mechanical analogue: exchange defines a conservative manifold, while production and financial activity appear as sources or sinks of value.

3. Goodwin’s Classical Growth Cycle

Goodwin’s (1967) model couples employment (\(\lambda\)) and wage share (\(\omega\)):

\[ \dot{\lambda} = \lambda(a - b\omega), \qquad \dot{\omega} = \omega(-c + d\lambda). \]

3.1 Hamiltonian representation

Setting \(q = \ln \lambda\) and \(p = \ln \omega\), we have:

\[ \dot q = a - b e^{p}, \qquad \dot p = -c + d e^{q}. \]

This admits the Hamiltonian:

\[ H(q,p) = a p - b e^{p} + c q - d e^{q}. \]

Hamilton’s equations follow:

\[ \dot q = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}. \]

Hence, \(dH/dt = 0\): the system is conservative. Level sets of \(H\) form closed orbits—oscillations that redistribute value between wages and profits without net creation.

3.2 Departures from conservation

Fiscal policy, credit, or depreciation introduce nonconservative terms (\(dH/dt \neq 0\)), representing creation or destruction of value beyond exchange. The Hamiltonian kernel describes pure value circulation; policy and finance act as external forces doing work on the system.

4. Flaschel–Semmler: Intersectoral Value Circulation

4.1 Dynamics

Let \(M = B - RA\) represent excess-demand and excess-profit couplings between sectors. The price–quantity adjustments are:

\[ \frac{\dot p}{p} = -\,\Delta_p M x, \qquad \frac{\dot x}{x} = \Delta_x M^\top p, \]

where \(\Delta_p, \Delta_x\) are positive diagonal matrices of adjustment speeds.

4.2 Hamiltonian structure

With \(y = \ln p\) and \(z = \ln x\):

\[ \dot y = -\,\Delta_p M e^{z}, \qquad \dot z = \Delta_x M^\top e^{y}. \]

When \(\Delta_p = \delta_p I\) and \(\Delta_x = \delta_x I\),

\[ H(z,y) = \delta_x \mathbf{1}^\top M e^{y} + \delta_p \mathbf{1}^\top M^\top e^{z}, \]

generates the dynamics through:

\[ \dot z = \frac{\partial H}{\partial y}, \qquad \dot y = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial z}, \qquad \Rightarrow \frac{dH}{dt}=0. \]

This Hamiltonian expresses conservation of total intersectoral value in exchange—an extension of Goodwin’s conservation law.

4.3 Power and nonconservative forces

When adjustment speeds differ or financial channels appear, the system becomes nonconservative. The rate of change of \(H\) is:

\[ \dot H = \sum_i \Pi_i^{(x)} - \sum_i \Pi_i^{(p)}, \]

where

\[ \Pi_i^{(x)} = \delta_x \delta_p x_i [(M^\top p)_i]^2, \qquad \Pi_i^{(p)} = \delta_x \delta_p p_i [(Mx)_i]^2. \]

The \(\Pi\) terms measure the sectoral power flow—the rate at which value is created or dissipated by asymmetries, finance, or policy.

5. Foley (1987): Dissipative Liquidity–Profit Cycles

Foley extends the classical circuit to include liquidity ratios (\(m,f\)) and profit rate (\(r\)). The system undergoes a Hopf bifurcation:

\[ \dot \rho = \mu \rho + \kappa \rho^3, \qquad \dot \theta = \omega_0 + O(\rho^2). \]

Defining the energy-like function \(E = \frac{1}{2}\rho^2\) yields:

\[ \dot E = \mu \rho^2 + \kappa \rho^4. \]

Here:

  • \(\mu \rho^2\) represents power injection (credit expansion, investment accelerators),
  • \(\kappa \rho^4\) represents dissipation (liquidity constraint, repayment).

The steady amplitude \(\rho^2 = -\mu/\kappa\) defines a stable limit cycle. Foley’s model is thus dissipative: finance temporarily violates value conservation, producing nominal cycles of creation and destruction before equilibrium is restored.

6. From Conservative Exchange to Dissipative Finance

These models trace the evolution from closed conservative circuits of classical exchange to open dissipative circuits under finance. Labor acts as a source of real value, while financial mechanisms create and destroy nominal value, introducing energy-like nonconservative forces into the macroeconomic system.

7. Mathematical Appendix

7.1 Goodwin Hamiltonian

Given \[ \dot{\lambda} = \lambda(a - b\omega), \qquad \dot{\omega} = \omega(-c + d\lambda), \] set \(q = \ln \lambda\), \(p = \ln \omega\). Then \[ \dot q = a - b e^{p}, \qquad \dot p = -c + d e^{q}, \] which integrates to the Hamiltonian \[ H(q,p) = a p - b e^{p} + c q - d e^{q}, \] with \(\dot q = \partial H / \partial p\) and \(\dot p = -\partial H / \partial q\). The conservation \(\dot H = 0\) holds in the absence of external forces.

7.2 Flaschel–Semmler Hamiltonian

For uniform adjustment speeds (\(\Delta_p = \delta_p I\), \(\Delta_x = \delta_x I\)), \[ \dot y = -\,\Delta_p M e^{z}, \qquad \dot z = \Delta_x M^\top e^{y}, \] with Hamiltonian \[ H(z,y) = \delta_x \mathbf{1}^\top M e^{y} + \delta_p \mathbf{1}^\top M^\top e^{z}. \] When speeds differ, \(\dot H \neq 0\), indicating nonconservative behavior: \[ \dot H = (M^\top p)^\top \Delta_x^\top \operatorname{diag}(x)(M^\top p) - (Mx)^\top \Delta_p^\top \operatorname{diag}(p)(Mx). \] This expression quantifies the net power generated or dissipated by asymmetries and financial interactions.

8. Conclusion

The Hamiltonian framework of Goodwin and Flaschel–Semmler captures the classical principle that exchange conserves value—it merely redistributes it among agents or sectors. Labor, as productive activity, acts as the source of new value. Finance, conversely, introduces nonconservative loops that create and annihilate value within the circulation of capital.

The unifying insight is that the economy functions as a hybrid dynamical system: partly conservative, governed by the law of value, and partly dissipative, driven by financial and policy forces. Classical value theory and modern system dynamics thus converge on a single principle: value behaves as an energy-like magnitude whose circulation, creation, and dissipation define the rhythm of capitalist reproduction.
